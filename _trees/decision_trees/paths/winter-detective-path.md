# Winter Detective Path

This path is for when the tree has no leaves, making identification more challenging.

```
## Bud Identification

Buds are key winter identification features:

### Bud Arrangement
├── OPPOSITE BUDS
│   ├── Buds directly across from each other
│   ├── Limited number of trees (remember "MAD Horse" - Maple, Ash, Dogwood, Horse-chestnut)
│   ├── Examples: Pacific Dogwood, Oregon Ash, Maple species
│   └── Check multiple branches to confirm pattern
│
├── ALTERNATE BUDS
│   ├── Buds staggered along branch
│   ├── Most common arrangement in deciduous trees
│   ├── Examples: Oak, Alder, Buckeye, Sycamore
│   └── Spacing and angle varies by species
│
└── WHORLED BUDS
    ├── Multiple buds emerge from same point
    ├── Uncommon in California trees
    ├── Often in specialized species
    └── Sometimes in modified branch structures

### Bud Characteristics
├── BUD SIZE
│   ├── LARGE BUDS (>1/4 inch)
│   │   ├── California Buckeye: Very large, pointed buds
│   │   ├── Walnut: Large, irregular buds with fuzzy covering
│   │   ├── Valley Oak: Clustered buds at twig tips
│   │   └── Magnolia: Fuzzy, pointed buds with stipule scars
│   │
│   └── SMALL BUDS (<1/4 inch)
│       ├── Birch: Small, pointed buds pressed against twig
│       ├── Madrone: Small, red, rounded buds
│       ├── Alder: Purplish buds on short stalks
│       └── Willow: Small, pressed against stem, covered by single scale
│
├── BUD COLOR
│   ├── RED BUDS
│   │   ├── Madrone: Bright red, clustered at branch tips
│   │   ├── Red Alder: Reddish-purple, stalked buds
│   │   └── Red Maple: Red, rounded, multiple scales
│   │
│   ├── BLACK/DARK BUDS
│   │   ├── Ash: Black, dome-shaped terminal buds
│   │   ├── Black Walnut: Gray-black with fuzzy covering
│   │   └── White Alder: Dark purple buds
│   │
│   ├── BROWN BUDS
│   │   ├── Oak species: Clustered at twig tips, pointed
│   │   ├── Bigleaf Maple: Blunt, greenish-brown
│   │   └── Dogwood: Button-like flower buds
│   │
│   └── GREEN BUDS
│       ├── California Laurel: Greenish buds, aromatic
│       ├── California Buckeye: Large greenish buds
│       └── Some Willow species: Green or yellowish buds
│
├── BUD SHAPE
│   ├── POINTED/CONICAL
│   │   ├── Beech: Long, slender, pointed buds
│   │   ├── Oak species: Clustered, pointed buds
│   │   └── Madrone: Red pointed buds
│   │
│   ├── ROUND/BLUNT
│   │   ├── Maple species: Rounded buds with multiple scales
│   │   ├── Ash: Dome-shaped terminal buds
│   │   └── Dogwood: Button-like flower buds
│   │
│   └── SPECIALIZED SHAPES
│       ├── California Buckeye: Large, pointed, sticky buds
│       ├── Sycamore: Buds hidden inside hollow leaf stalks
│       └── Western Redbud: Multiple tiny buds clustered together
│
└── BUD COVERINGS
    ├── SCALY BUDS (multiple overlapping scales)
    │   ├── Oak: Multiple overlapping scales
    │   ├── Maple: Several pairs of scales
    │   └── Ash: Several pairs of scales
    │
    ├── NAKED BUDS (no scales, just folded leaves)
    │   ├── California Buckeye: Large, sticky naked buds
    │   ├── Walnut: Partially naked buds showing folded leaves
    │   └── Witchhazel: Naked fuzzy buds
    │
    └── SINGLE-SCALE BUDS (covered by one cap-like scale)
        ├── Willow: Single hood-like scale
        ├── Sycamore: Single, cap-like scale
        └── Buttonbush: Single pointed scale

## Twig Identification

Twigs provide reliable winter identification clues:

### Twig Color
├── RED OR REDDISH TWIGS
│   ├── Dogwood: Bright red in some species
│   ├── Red Osier Dogwood: Distinctive bright red
│   ├── Red Alder: Reddish-brown with white lenticels
│   └── Madrone: Smooth reddish twigs
│
├── BROWN TWIGS (most common)
│   ├── Oak: Gray-brown with clustered buds at tips
│   ├── Maple: Brown with opposite buds
│   ├── Alder: Brown with catkins in winter
│   └── Walnut: Thick brown twigs with chambered pith
│
├── GREEN TWIGS
│   ├── California Buckeye: Stout green to gray twigs
│   ├── Willow: Young twigs often green or yellowish
│   └── Elderberry: Green twigs with white pith
│
└── GRAY TWIGS
    ├── Aspen: Gray to whitish smooth twigs
    ├── Birch: Gray with white lenticels
    └── Ash: Stout gray twigs with opposite buds

### Twig Features
├── PITH CHARACTERISTICS (center of twig when cut)
│   ├── CHAMBERED PITH (divided into sections)
│   │   ├── Walnut: Brown pith divided into chambers
│   │   └── Distinctive identification feature
│   │
│   ├── HOLLOW PITH
│   │   ├── Elderberry: Large white pith, partially hollow
│   │   ├── Some Honeysuckle: Hollow brown pith
│   │   └── Check by cutting twig lengthwise
│   │
│   ├── COLORED PITH
│   │   ├── Elderberry: White pith
│   │   ├── Box-elder: Salmon-colored pith
│   │   └── Sumac: Brown pith
│   │
│   └── DIAPHRAGMED PITH (solid with spaced plates)
│       ├── Black Walnut: Brown diaphragmed pith
│       ├── Hackberry: White diaphragmed pith
│       └── Magnolia: Diaphragmed pith with gaps
│
├── LENTICELS (air pores on twigs)
│   ├── PROMINENT LENTICELS
│   │   ├── Birch: Horizontal white lenticels
│   │   ├── Cherry: Horizontal orange-brown lenticels
│   │   └── Alder: White elongated lenticels
│   │
│   └── INCONSPICUOUS LENTICELS
│       ├── Oak: Small scattered lenticels
│       ├── Maple: Small raised lenticels
│       └── Ash: Scattered dot-like lenticels
│
├── LEAF SCARS (where leaves detached)
│   ├── LARGE, DISTINCTIVE SCARS
│   │   ├── California Buckeye: Large shield-shaped scars with multiple bundle scars
│   │   ├── Walnut: Shield-shaped with 3-lobed bundle scar pattern
│   │   ├── Ash: Large U-shaped or crescent scars
│   │   └── Kentucky Coffeetree: Large heart-shaped scars
│   │
│   ├── SMALL OR NONDESCRIPT SCARS
│   │   ├── Oak: Small half-round scars
│   │   ├── Maple: Small U-shaped, narrow scars
│   │   └── Birch: Small triangular scars
│   │
│   └── BUNDLE SCARS (dots inside leaf scars)
│       ├── 3 BUNDLE SCARS
│       │   ├── Maple: 3 distinct bundle scars
│       │   ├── Oak: 3-many scattered bundle scars
│       │   └── Most common arrangement
│       │
│       ├── MANY BUNDLE SCARS
│       │   ├── Walnut: Many bundle scars in U-shape
│       │   ├── Ash: Many bundle scars in crescent
│       │   └── Sycamore: Many bundle scars in ring
│       │
│       └── RING OF BUNDLE SCARS
│           ├── Sycamore: Complete ring of bundle scars
│           ├── Basswood: Bundle scars in ring
│           └── Distinctive feature when present
│
└── STIPULE SCARS (small scars beside leaf scars)
    ├── PROMINENT STIPULE SCARS
    │   ├── Magnolia: Ring-like scars around twig
    │   ├── Sycamore: Ring-like scars at nodes
    │   └── Black Willow: Distinct stipule scars
    │
    └── NO STIPULE SCARS
        ├── Most conifers: No stipule scars
        ├── Oak: Typically no stipule scars
        └── Absent in most trees

## Branch and Crown Patterns

Overall structure provides winter identification clues:

### Branching Patterns
├── OPPOSITE BRANCHING
│   ├── Branches grow directly across from each other
│   ├── Examples: Maple, Ash, Dogwood
│   ├── Relatively uncommon pattern - only a few genera
│   └── Creates symmetrical appearance
│
├── ALTERNATE BRANCHING
│   ├── Branches staggered along trunk
│   ├── Most common pattern (about 80% of trees)
│   ├── Examples: Oak, Birch, Sycamore
│   └── Often creates less symmetrical appearance
│
├── WHORLED BRANCHING
│   ├── Multiple branches radiating from same point
│   ├── Examples: Pine family often has whorled pattern
│   ├── Particularly visible in young trees
│   └── Creates distinctive layered appearance
│
└── SPECIALIZED BRANCHING
    ├── ZIGZAG TWIGS
    │   ├── Elm: Distinctive zigzag pattern
    │   ├── Hackberry: Similar zigzag pattern
    │   └── Chinese Elm: Pronounced zigzag twigs
    │
    ├── SPUR BRANCHES (short, stubby branches)
    │   ├── Apple/Pear: Short fruit-bearing spurs
    │   ├── Beech: Short spur branches
    │   └── Important identification feature
    │
    └── THORNS AND SPINES
        ├── Black Locust: Paired spines at nodes
        ├── Hawthorn: Long sharp thorns
        └── Honey Locust: Branched thorns on trunk

### Crown Silhouettes
└── See the [Silhouette Detective Path](silhouette-detective-path.md) for detailed information on winter tree profiles

## Winter Persistent Features

Look for remaining clues when leaves are gone:

### Persistent Fruits/Seeds
├── HANGING FRUITS
│   ├── Sycamore: Round spiky balls on long stalks
│   ├── Catalpa: Long bean-like pods
│   ├── Sweetgum: Spiky round seed balls
│   └── Ash: Hanging clusters of single-winged seeds
│
├── NUTS/SEEDS ON GROUND
│   ├── Oak: Acorns and cups
│   ├── Buckeye: Large round nuts with leathery husks
│   ├── Walnut: Round nuts with green to black husks
│   └── Check tree base for fallen reproductive parts
│
└── PERSISTENT CONES
    ├── Pine: Various cone sizes/shapes by species
    ├── Alder: Small woody "cones" persist through winter
    ├── Birch: Small catkin-like cones
    └── Douglas-fir: Distinctive cones with "mouse tails"

### Bark Features
└── See the [Bark Detective Path](bark-detective-path.md) for detailed information on bark identification

## Winter Tree ID Practice Tips

1. Start with the most obvious features: opposite vs. alternate branching
2. Examine buds closely - size, shape, arrangement, color
3. Look for persistent fruits or seeds on the tree or ground
4. Check for distinctive twig features (color, lenticels, pith)
5. Observe the overall silhouette and branching pattern
6. Note habitat and growing location for context clues
7. Use multiple characteristics to confirm your identification

Winter identification becomes easier with practice. Take photos of trees in winter and summer to build your recognition skills across seasons.
```
